Do it tomorrow.

I've finished reading Mark Forster's "Do it Tomorrow", a book that presents an approach to using time effectively, achieving goals, and having focus.  If I'm being uncharitable, there's a bit of coaching flimflam to get through early on ("Effectiveness = Creativity x Order", gah), but this 200 page book is a quick and easy read with some great ideas in it.

To be clear: I'm not shopping around for the perfect time management methodology.  I'm interested in practical, sane, useful techniques that I feel would work for me, and I've found a few in here. Quite a few.

What I took away from the book is that there's a surprising amount you can put off, and by doing so you can stay focussed on what you're doing today.   Putting things off until tomorrow doesn't mean you're being slack.  It means you're giving what you want to do today the attention it needs to get it done.

What can be put off? There are no absolute rules, and you need judgement and common sense to decide if you can simply note down a new action for tomorrow, or if it is one of the rare things that you really do need to handle today.

A good example is post (for those of us who still get some).  Open it, but then put it in an in-tray.  You know what it is, and if it's genuinely urgent, you can deal with it.  But otherwise, it's there waiting for you tomorrow, when you can include it in your plan for that day.

Forster technologies are a diary and a closed list.  The distinction to a to-do list that he makes is that to-do lists just accumulate.  A closed list is the list of things you will do, and you draw a line under it: nothing else gets added today (subject to common sense).  Every day, you complete your closed list. If you don't, well that happens sometimes, but if it happens a lot you stop, move everything into a back-log, start clean, and begin making progress.

There are plenty of other details and ideas in the book, but the last one I'll mention is the "current initiative".  This is the goal you want to achieve, and you do something on it every day as the first thing you do.  No excuses.  If you don't progress your current initiative (whatever it is), it's not going to happen. Doing even a small something every day means you are doing something, progressing the initiative, and by doing it first, it's not going to just slip off the radar and get lost in all the other stuff you do.

It appears the author has moved on to other techniques (Autofocus), but there's some nice ideas in DiT.  I've found it helpful and restorative.
